# Danish government formation: institutional foundations

This document describes how Danish government formation works as a system. It covers constitutional rules, procedural norms, and the informal institutions that shape coalition politics. The content is time-invariant or slowly changing and serves as the reference manual for the coalition simulator.

---

## 1. Constitutional framework

Four provisions of the Grundloven (enacted 5 June 1953) govern the life and death of Danish governments. The constitutional text is sparse -- barely one hundred words across the relevant sections -- and the gaps are filled by political practice (politisk praksis) that scholars explicitly distinguish from binding constitutional convention (forfatningsretlig saedvane).

**Paragraph 14 -- ministerial appointment.** The King appoints and dismisses the PM and other ministers, determines their number, and distributes portfolio responsibilities. The incoming PM countersigns their own appointment and simultaneously signs the outgoing PM's dismissal. This is a distinctive Danish feature: the monarchical formality is real, but the substantive decision lies entirely with the political actors. Combined with section 12 (the King exercises powers only through ministers) and section 13 (the King bears no personal responsibility), the monarch provides the constitutional vessel for a decision controlled entirely by politicians.

**Paragraph 15 -- removal by no-confidence vote.** No minister may remain in office after the Folketing has expressed no confidence. If the Folketing expresses no confidence in the PM specifically, the PM must request the entire ministry's resignation unless new elections are called. A caretaker government continues in office, limited to what is necessary for uninterrupted conduct of official business. The word "mistillid" must appear in the proposal's formulation for the constitutional obligation to attach. A kabinetsspoergsmaal (cabinet question) -- where the government pre-announces that defeat on a particular vote triggers resignation -- is a political instrument, not a constitutional requirement.

**Paragraph 32 stk. 2 -- the presentation requirement.** After a new ministry is appointed, the PM cannot call new elections before presenting to the Folketing. This prevents a newly appointed PM from immediately dissolving parliament to avoid a confidence test.

**Paragraph 35 -- convening after election.** The newly elected Folketing convenes at noon on the twelfth weekday after election day, unless the King has summoned it earlier. There is no constitutional deadline for government formation; the caretaker government simply continues under section 15 restrictions.

**Paragraphs 45-46 -- the budget.** The government must present the finanslovsforslag at least four months before the fiscal year. No taxes may be collected and no expenditure incurred without appropriation authority from a passed finanslov, tillaegbevillingslov, or midlertidig bevillingslov.

## 2. Negative parliamentarism

Denmark operates the purest form of negative parliamentarism among established democracies. A government needs no parliamentary vote to take office -- it must merely avoid a demonstrated majority against it.

**No investiture vote.** Denmark has no tiltraedelsesafstemning. This is not merely convention but flows from the constitutional architecture: section 14 vests appointment power in the monarch, and section 15 prescribes only how governments are removed, not installed. The Folketing as a body has no formal role in approving a new government.

**Required majority for removal.** A mistillidsvotum requires a simple majority of those present and voting, not an absolute majority of all 179 members. The quorum requirement under section 52 mandates that at least half the members be present. The 1975 vote -- the most recent successful no-confidence motion -- passed 86-85 with 171 members present.

**Practical significance.** A government needs only to not have 90 votes actively cast against it, not to have 90 votes supporting it. Abstentions work in the government's favor. This creates structural space for parties that neither support nor oppose a government. Analysis drawing on publicly available expert commentary emphasizes that this constitutional logic is the single most important structural feature for understanding Danish coalition formation.

**Comparison with Scandinavia.** Sweden's 1974 Regeringsformen transferred government formation to the Speaker and requires a formal vote, though using a negative investiture rule (the nomination fails only if an absolute majority votes against it). Norway has no investiture vote either, but the stortingspresident has a consultative role. Denmark combines all features of maximum negativity: no vote, no Speaker role, no codified process, and an outgoing PM who controls the entire procedure. As Bergman (1993) documented, this configuration correlates strongly with minority government frequency.

**The Schlueter-era limit case (1982-1988).** The Schlueter governments were outvoted repeatedly on foreign and security policy by "alternative flertal" but stayed in office by refusing to designate those votes as cabinet questions. This demonstrates that a government can survive repeated policy defeats without treating them as confidence matters -- the distinction between confidence viability and legislative viability is real and politically exploitable.

**Individual ministers vs. the whole government.** Section 15 stk. 1 covers any minister; stk. 2 adds the specific consequence for the PM. In practice, no individual minister has ever been formally removed by a mistillidsvotum. Ministers resign voluntarily (or are dismissed by the PM) before matters reach a vote.

**Any single member may propose a mistillidsvotum.** This was demonstrated in October 2025 when Lars Boje Mathiesen, sole representative of Borgernes Parti, filed Beslutningsforslag B 16 against PM Frederiksen. The motion failed -- left-wing parties chose abstention over joining the borgerlige bloc.

**Three successful no-confidence votes since 1901:** 1909 (Holstein-Ledreborg), 1947 (Knud Kristensen, who chose to call elections rather than resign), and 1975 (Poul Hartling, 86-85). The rarity is itself informative: the shadow of the mistillidsvotum disciplines governments far more often than its actual deployment.

## 3. The kongerunde

The kongerunde has no official name, no statutory basis, and no fixed rules. Authoritative constitutional scholarship describes it as a factual phenomenon, not a legal one. The process can be traced to 1909 and was institutionalized from 1926. The term dronningerunde entered usage around 1975 under Margrethe II; following the accession of Frederik X in January 2024, kongerunde has become the current term.

**Three governing rules.** Analysis drawing on publicly available expert commentary identifies exactly three rules that constrain government formation:

1. The process is conducted on the outgoing PM's responsibility. The sitting PM decides whether to hold a kongerunde, how to interpret the parties' advice, who gets appointed as forhandlingsleder, and what mandate they receive. The PM is under no legal obligation to hold a kongerunde at all.

2. No PM may be appointed who would be expected to face an immediate mistillidsvotum. The task is to present a government to the Folketing that will not immediately be met with a no-confidence vote.

3. The incoming PM countersigns their own appointment.

**Critically, none of these three rules require that a kongerunde take place.** The kongerunde is functionally a ceremony. If the parliamentary situation is clear, the sitting PM can simply continue governing and negotiating, as has happened historically on multiple occasions.

**Sequence of events.** After an election or government resignation: (1) The sitting PM visits the monarch to report the parliamentary situation. (2) If the PM determines that no majority supports the government's continuation, the PM submits a resignation and recommends a kongerunde. (3) Representatives of all parties with Folketing seats visit the monarch at Amalienborg in order of party size, communicating recommendations both orally and in writing. (4) Based on the parties' recommendations, the monarch appoints a forhandlingsleder. (5) The forhandlingsleder conducts negotiations and reports back.

**The monarch exercises no independent judgment.** The monarch has no genuine competence, only a formal role. The Easter Crisis of 1920 -- when Christian X dismissed PM Zahle's government despite it retaining a Folketing majority -- was the last time a Danish monarch attempted independent political action. The resulting crisis permanently cemented the convention of royal non-interference.

**Forhandlingsleder, not "kongelig undersoeger."** The term kongelig undersoeger (royal investigator) is a journalistic designation, not an official one. The distinction between an "investigator" and a "formateur" is not one of different roles but of mandate scope: some forhandlingsledere receive a clear mandate to form a government, while others are tasked with investigating possibilities.

**The Speaker plays no role.** Unlike in Sweden (where the talman runs the entire formation process) or Norway (where the stortingspresident is formally consulted), Denmark's Folketingets formand has no function in government formation.

**Parties' advice can be strategic.** Constitutional scholarship notes that parties' recommendations may be driven by varied considerations, including wanting to buy time, sideline other parties, or throw sand in the machinery. Analysis drawing on publicly available expert commentary emphasizes that if parties point to an alternative forhandlingsleder, this does not prevent the sitting PM from conducting parallel negotiations. Once a forhandlingsleder is appointed, they can negotiate with whomever they choose about whatever they choose -- the negotiations are not confined to a single room or process.

**Multiple rounds.** The January-February 1975 formation required four dronningerunder -- the most in Danish history. The 2022 SVM formation took 44 days. There is no constitutional maximum for formation time.

## 4. The formateur/forhandlingsleder role

The forhandlingsleder is appointed by the monarch on the basis of party recommendations in the kongerunde. Historical cases illustrate the range of outcomes:

- **1975:** Speaker Karl Skytte (Radikale) was appointed as a neutral forhandlingsleder -- explicitly not a PM candidate -- to investigate broad coalition options. Four rounds were needed before Anker Joergensen formed a government.
- **1982:** Anker Joergensen resigned without election after losing Radikale support. A majority pointed directly to Poul Schlueter as forhandlingsleder. One round.
- **1988:** The record-holder -- four rounds with three different forhandlingsledere.
- **2015:** Thorning-Schmidt herself advised the Queen that a majority pointed to Loekke. Two rounds were needed as Loekke first attempted a four-party coalition, then formed V-alone with 34 mandates.
- **2022:** Despite Loekke's attempts to complicate the process, Moderaterne ultimately pointed to Frederiksen, giving her a mandate-majority. Negotiations lasted six weeks.

**The sitting PM controls the process.** This gives the incumbent enormous procedural advantage. The only scenario where the formateur mandate goes elsewhere is when the parliamentary situation has unambiguously shifted. No post-war kongerunde has produced a PM from the side with fewer mandates pointing to them.

## 5. Finanslov as the real confidence mechanism

Although no formal constitutional provision designates the annual finanslov as a confidence test, its functional equivalence is unambiguous. A government cannot function without appropriation authority, and rejection of its budget is understood as a fundamental withdrawal of parliamentary support.

**The annual cycle.** Ministries prepare budgets from January through June; the finanslovsforslag is presented in August; first reading occurs in September; political negotiations begin in late October with the saettemoedet; intensive bargaining occurs in November; third reading and final vote happen in December.

**The midlertidig bevillingslov safety valve.** When the finanslov cannot be completed before the fiscal year begins, a provisional appropriations act must be introduced. There exists a strong norm across nearly all parties to vote for provisional appropriations -- a norm traceable to the 1894 settlement that ended the Estrup-era constitutional crisis.

**Two post-war rejections.** In 1929, the Madsen-Mygdal government lost its budget when De Konservative refused to vote for it. In 1983, the Schlueter government's budget was rejected when Fremskridtspartiet (which refused on principle to vote for any finanslov), Socialdemokratiet, and SF voted against. Schlueter called elections and won decisively.

**The finanslovsnormen.** Since 1921, the four "old responsible parties" (S, V, KF, RV) have maintained a convention of voting FOR the final finanslov at third reading even from opposition. KF broke this norm only three times (1925, 1929, 1976). This means KF routinely votes FOR S-led finanslove at third reading, but this carries zero policy content -- only joining an actual finanslovsaftale (budget agreement) would be meaningful.

**Governments can and do pass the finanslov with parties outside their formal support base.** The VK governments (2001-2011) typically passed narrow agreements solely with DF, while the SVM government's 2024 finanslov was backed by 163 mandates.

## 6. The support-party spectrum

Danish parliamentary practice recognizes a continuum of relationships between a government and non-government parties, from full coalition membership to passive non-opposition. The critical insight is that these categories shade into one another along what scholars call a "sliding transition."

**Regeringsparti** (coalition party): receives ministerial portfolios, bound by the collectively negotiated regeringsgrundlag. All legislation must be internally agreed before presentation.

**Stoetteparti with forstaelsespapir**: a non-government party that commits via a signed document to sustain the government in exchange for policy commitments. The sole national-level example is the 2019 arrangement between S and RV, SF, and EL -- a genuinely novel innovation.

**Stoetteparti without written agreement**: DF's arrangement with VK governments from 2001 to 2011. Influence flowed through annual finanslov negotiations and ad hoc policy deals, without any published document.

**Fast stoetteparti vs. loest stoetteparti**: a recognized analytical distinction. Fixed support parties generally back the government; loose support parties cooperate on specific issues. Minority governments with a stable majority support base use a logrolling strategy; those without stable support are relegated to compromise.

**Passive toleration**: integral to negative parliamentarism. A party neither supports nor opposes. The canonical example is Det Radikale Venstre under the Schlueter governments (1982-1988): RV supported economic policy but voted against on security/foreign affairs without ever filing a no-confidence motion.

**Vekslende flertal** (changing majorities): governing by assembling different coalitions for different issues. The Hartling government (1973-75) with 22 Venstre seats is the textbook case.

**The forstaelsespapir innovation.** The 2019 document "Retfaerdig retning for Danmark" was the first national government-formation document negotiated between a government party and its external support parties. It has no legal force -- the sanction for violation is purely political. The distinction from a regeringsgrundlag is politically deliberate: a forstaelsespapir sets political direction while preserving government autonomy on detail.

**Support parties' power is concentrated in the pre-formation phase.** Analysis drawing on publicly available expert commentary emphasizes that once a government is seated, it can seek shifting majorities and is no longer dependent on the precise terms initially negotiated. This is why parties increasingly insist on detailed written agreements before formation rather than trusting post-formation negotiation.

## 7. Support-party leverage and the structural trap

Danish support parties exercise ongoing influence through multiple mechanisms: annual finanslov negotiations (the most powerful lever), establishing public "roede linjer" (red lines), threatening to "traekke stikket" (pull the plug), and issue-specific blocking or demanding. The fundamental dilemma is structural: a support party that topples "its own" government in a bloc-polarized system almost certainly hands power to the opposing bloc -- a consequence support parties find intolerable despite deep frustration.

**No support party has ever successfully toppled a Scandinavian government -- until 2021.** The Swedish exception came when Vaensterpartiet toppled PM Loefven via mistillidsvotum over rent liberalization -- the first such event in Scandinavian history. The structural explanation for the rarity: exit threats are asymmetrically incredible. A support party can declare itself "no longer a support party" (as EL did in 2012) while continuing to vote case-by-case, including signing the next year's finanslovsaftale. The practical mechanics exist on a spectrum: rhetorical withdrawal, ultimatum with deadline (RV's 2022 slow-motion mistillidsvotum that forced Frederiksen to call an election), refusal to vote for the finanslov, and the nuclear option of filing a formal mistillidsvotum.

**The aftaleparlamentarisme system.** As of 2021, Finansministeriet counted 116 active forlig (legislative agreements). Approximately 80% of pre-2022 legislative agreements crossed bloc lines. Broad agreements survive government changes; narrow agreements are vulnerable to electoral shifts. This creates a dense web of commitments that any new government inherits and must navigate. The distinction between brede forlig (broad, cross-bloc agreements) and smalle forlig (narrow, within-bloc agreements) is directly relevant to government viability.

**Domain-specific legislative coalitions.** A government can win on economics while losing on foreign policy. The Schlueter governments (1982-93) exemplify this: the Firkloeverregering relied on Radikale Venstre for economic policy while an "alternative security-policy majority" (S, RV, SF, VS) overrode the government on security, producing the extraordinary fodnotepolitik -- 22-28 resolutions adopted against the government's wishes, registered as footnotes in NATO communiques. The system functioned because RV separated its economic support from its foreign-policy opposition. This structural feature means the distinction between confidence viability and legislative viability is real and consequential.

## 8. Minority government as the Danish default

Denmark holds the highest occurrence of minority governments in the world. Only five post-war coalition governments have enjoyed a parliamentary majority. No single party has held an absolute Folketing majority since the first decade of the twentieth century.

**Structural causes.** Four factors interact: (1) negative parliamentarism, which eliminates the requirement to demonstrate a majority; (2) a highly proportional electoral system preventing any single party from gaining a majority; (3) a multi-party system (typically 8-13 parties); and (4) a strong committee system and forlig culture giving opposition parties policy influence, creating what Stroem (1990) called the policy-influence-versus-office trade-off.

**Manoevredygtighed** (maneuverability) is central to practitioner discourse. Minority government gives significant room because the government can work with different parties on different issues. This is a key reason Danish parties often prefer minority governments: a majority coalition constrains all partners to negotiate only internally. Paradoxically, even the SVM majority government maintained maneuverability norms, forming legislative coalitions with other parties and not passing any measure only with its own votes.

**The extreme case: Hartling (1973-75).** With just 22 Venstre mandates (12% of seats), Hartling navigated budget passage through shifting coalitions: the Februarforliget (February 1974, with S, CD, KrF) and Store Bededagsforliget (May 1974, with K, CD, KrF, and even Fremskridtspartiet). Nearly one-third of all legislation during Hartling's tenure was introduced by non-government parties. He used the threat of calling elections as his primary leverage against reluctant partners. The government lasted 14 months before falling on an 86-85 mistillidsvotum.

## 9. PM selection norms

The Constitution is silent on PM selection. The operative norm is that the leader of the largest party within the winning bloc becomes PM. This norm is strong but not inviolable, and it tracks the bloc's anchor party rather than the Folketing's largest party overall.

**Two exceptions.** Hilmar Baunsgaard (Det Radikale Venstre) led the VKR government (1968-71) despite RV being the smallest coalition party. Poul Schlueter (Det Konservative Folkeparti) became PM in 1982 -- the first Conservative PM since 1901 -- not because his party was largest, but because he was the politician who could assemble a viable coalition. After the 1990 election, Schlueter continued because Radikale "did not want Auken as prime minister" -- demonstrating that coalition partners' preferences about individuals can override seat arithmetic.

**The 2015 precedent.** Venstre won only 34 seats -- third behind S (47) and DF (37) -- yet Loekke became PM because the blue bloc collectively won a majority and V was the acknowledged anchor party with the declared statsministerkandidat.

**The statsministerkandidat concept** is entirely informal with no legal or constitutional basis. It arose in the 1990s when TV stations began arranging debates between declared PM candidates, and has acquired practical significance by structuring pre-election expectations.

## 10. Government size norms

Denmark massively deviates from Riker's (1962) minimum winning coalition prediction. Most governments are not even winning coalitions -- they are undersized minorities. Government parties' seat shares range from Hartling's extreme 12% to typical minority coalitions holding 33-47% of seats. Majority governments are rare.

**Typical configuration:** one to three parties. No government has had more than four parties. Single-party minority governments and two-party coalitions are the most common configurations. Skjaeveland's (2009) Dual Power Base Model -- combining the median voter theorem with an "Office Capacity" constraint -- explains 23 of 25 Danish government formations between 1953 and 2007.

**Oversized coalitions are extremely rare.** The SVM government started with 89 domestic mandates (49.7%), reaching an effective majority only with North Atlantic seats.

## 11. Cross-bloc precedents

The SVM government (2022) was widely characterized as a historic norm-break, but it has identifiable predecessors:

- **SV government (1978-79):** Anker Joergensen (S) with Venstre's Henning Christophersen. 86 mandates, lasted 14 months. Plagued by internal "shadow ministries."
- **Trekantsregering (1957-60):** H.C. Hansen (S) with RV and the Georgist Retsforbund.
- **VKR government (1968-71):** Cross-bloc because RV had traditionally been S's coalition partner.
- **KVR government (1988-90):** Schlueter again crossed the traditional line by including RV.
- **Nyrup I (1993-94):** S, RV, CD, and KrF -- included centrist/centre-right parties.

The bloc terminology itself was coined by Uffe Ellemann-Jensen in 1994 and became mainstream around 2011. Within this framework, S and V governing together was genuinely unprecedented in living memory. Analysis drawing on publicly available expert commentary warns that a failed cross-bloc government carries severe electoral consequences: junior partners in broad governments are historically destroyed, and the perception of governing incompetence sets the responsible parties back significantly in voters' eyes.
